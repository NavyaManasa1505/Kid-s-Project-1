India is a land of festivals. A festival is a time for fun, eating sweets and special dishes wearing new clothes and meeting relatives and friends. The festivals that we celebrate can be grouped into three kinds. They are national festivals, harvest festivals and religious festivals.

NATIONAL FESTIVALS

The festivals which are celebrated by all the citizens of the country are known as national festivals. Republic Day, Independence Day and Gandhi Jayanti are national festivals.

  • Republic Day: Republic Day is celebrated on 26 January every year. On this day, the president hoists the national flag at Rajpath, New Delhi. To celebrate this day, a Republic Day parade is held at the Rajpath, New Delhi. The President takes the salute of the three armed force-the Army, the Navy and the Air force.
  • Independence Day: Independence Day is celebrated on 15 August. On this day, in the year 1947, India became free from British Rule. The Prime Minister hoists the flag and addresses the nation from the Red Fort in Delhi. On this day, we remember those who fought for the freedom of our country. Schools and government offices host special programmes to celebrate this day with great zeal.
  • Gandhi Jayanti: Gandhi Jayanti is celebrated on 2nd October to mark the birthday of the Father of the Nation, Mahatma Gandhi. A prayer meeting is held at Raj Ghat, the samadhi of Mahatma Gandhi. Similar meetings are held all over the country.

HARVEST FESTIVALS

Harvest festivals are celebrated all over the country with great pomp to mark the harvesting of crops and the change of seasons. Let's know about some harvest festivals celebrated in India.

  • Basant Panchami: Basant Panchami marks the onset of the spring season. The goddess of learning, music and wisdom, Devi Saraswati is worshipped on this day by many people. On this day, people wear a yellow coloured dresses. Yellow is the colour of happiness, sunshine and warmth.
  • Makar Sankranti: Makar Sankranti is celebrated on 14 January to celebrate the harvest of winter crops. It is celebrated all over India but is known by different names. In Gujarat, Makar Sankranti is also known as Uttarayan whereas in Tamil Nadu, it is known as Pongal. On this day, people fly kites.
  • Lohri: Lohri is celebrated in Punjab on 13 January every year a day before Makar Sankranti. It celebrates the harvesting of winter crops. Bonfire is an important part of this festival. People throw sesame seeds, jaggery, peanuts and popcorn into the bonfire. They also sing and dance around it.

RELIGIOUS FESTIVALS

The festivals related to a particular religion are called religious festivals. In India, people celebrate all festivals, no matter which religion they follow. Communal harmony develops with these festivals. Some religious festivals are Dussehra, Durga Puja, Diwali, Eid, Janmashtami, Holi, Eid and Gurpurab. Each festival is celebrated in a unique way.

  • Dussehra: Dussehra is celebrated all over the country. It is also known as Vijayadashami. It celebrates the victory of good over evil. Ram Lila is performed during Dussehra. In some states such as Gujarat, it is known as Navratri.
  • Diwali: Diwali or Deepawali is the festival of lights. It is marked as the beginning of a new year in many states. People decorate their houses with diyas, candles and lights. Goddess Lakshmi and Lord Ganesha are Dival worshipped on Diwali.
  • Holi: Holi is the festival of colours. It is celebrated in March. People throw coloured powder, called gulal and splash water on one another.
  • Eid-ul-Fitr: Eid-ul-Fitr is an important festival celebrated by Muslims worldwide. It marks the end of Ramzan, the holy month of fasting. People pray together in Mosque. After the Eid namaz they wish each other 'Eid Mubarak'. They wear new clothes and eat sweets and a variety of dishes. Gifts are exchanged and people go to visit their relatives.
  • Christmas: Christmas is celebrated on 25 December to mark the birth of Jesus Christ. On this day, people go to the church. They decorate the Christmas tree in Their houses and exchange gifts. Children wait for Santa Claus to bring them gifts on Christmas eve.
